Want This as a Permanent Reduction Instead?

Instead of a check or a one-time credit, this same benefit can be applied directly to your mortgage principal — permanently lowering your breakeven rent so you don't need a new credit every year to sustain the lower rent.

Principal Buydown — The Permanent Fix

Instead of a check or a tax credit you have to requalify for every year, this applies your sized benefit directly to your mortgage principal — paid to your lender, not to you. Your breakeven rent permanently drops. Next year you don't need a new credit to sustain the same lower rent.

This is also why it works for everyone regardless of income: it isn't a deduction against income or a credit against a tax bill — it's a direct payment to your loan servicer, the same way any extra principal payment works today.

For Buyers — Inheriting or Renewing a Commitment

A principal curtailment is permanent to the loan it was applied to — but the loan gets paid off when a property sells. To make the affordability commitment outlast a sale, it has to be recorded against the property itself as a deed covenant, the same way an HOA restriction or easement survives a sale.

If you're buying a property with an existing covenant, you inherit the obligation to honor it for the remaining term — but you don't automatically get a new curtailment. That only happens if you choose to extend the commitment on your own new loan.

What This Does for the Property Long-Term

Every time an owner renews instead of letting the covenant lapse, this unit stays counted as permanently-or-near-permanently affordable housing stock in your district's Community Impact tracker — instead of reverting to market rent and disappearing from the program the moment it changes hands.

Community Impact — Where the Tax Dollars Actually Go

Every time someone uses this system to record a principal curtailment, it's tracked here, by district — not as an abstract rebate total, but as real numbers: how much mortgage debt got permanently extinguished, how many units stayed affordable across a sale, and how many lapsed back to market rent.

This is the answer to "what did my tax dollars actually do." Not a number disappearing into a general fund — a running, visible ledger tied to your own neighborhood.

How District Funds Could Route to Local Projects

In a real implementation, a small administrative share of the program (separate from the curtailment itself, which always goes to the participating landlord's own loan) could be earmarked for visible capital improvements in the same district where participation happened — roads, schools, parks. The logic: a community participating heavily in affordability preservation should see some of the program's footprint show up as something they can point to.
